The Bear Game

The instructor calls out positions in rapid sequence. Bear. Ninja. Turtle. Cat. The children have to react immediately — no hesitation, no thinking, just move. The room is laughing and moving and completely engaged. Underneath the laughter, every single child is drilling the most important skill in ground fighting: kinesthetic awareness.

Protect the Castle

The child is on their back, feet up, hands ready. The instructor tries to get past their feet and hands. The child's job is to keep them out using their legs and arms together. This is Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu guard defense — the same skill Sodiq uses to maintain position in his UFC fights. The child does not know they are learning guard defense. They know they are protecting their castle.

Choke the Instructor

The instructor crawls away as fast as they can. The children chase, jump on the back, and wrap their arms around the instructor's neck — exactly like a guillotine choke. The same submission Sodiq used to finish Don Shainis in 30 seconds in the UFC. By the end of the drill every child has practiced the guillotine choke twenty times without feeling like they practiced anything at all.

Spin and Backpack

The coach shoots in low. The child sprawls. But the coach still has one leg. Now the child has to spin around and end up on the coach's back like a backpack. This is the sprawl-and-spin to back take — one of the most important sequences in Mixed Martial Arts. Sodiq's 70 percent UFC takedown defense rate is built on exactly these kinds of transitions.
