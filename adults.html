A Bold and Personal Message From Sodiq Yusuff

Dear Future Champion,

My name is Sodiq Yusuff, and most people know me as "Super" from the UFC. But right now, I am not writing to you as a fighter. I am writing to you as a coach, a neighbor, and a man who believes that martial arts can change lives... because it changed mine.

Let me take you back for a moment.

When I was 9 years old, my mother made the hardest decision of her life. She left Nigeria, left 14 of my brothers and sisters behind, and brought only me and my younger brother to America. We landed in Maryland. No money. No connections. No safety net. Just a mother's belief that her boys deserved a shot at something better.

Those first years were rough. I was the immigrant kid. The one with the funny name that teachers couldn't pronounce. I got picked on. I got into fights. And it was in one of those early fights that I discovered something that would shape the rest of my life: the power of standing up for yourself. Not the power to hurt people. The power to earn respect. The power to walk through the world knowing you can handle whatever comes your way.

In fifth grade, I was flipping through channels and landed on Spike TV. The Ultimate Fighter was on. I watched these athletes... and as a kid who grew up on anime like Fullmetal Alchemist and Hunter x Hunter, I saw something I recognized immediately. These were real-life anime characters. Right there, sitting on my couch in PG County, Maryland, I told my mom: "That's what I want to do when I grow up."

Now, About Convincing a Nigerian Mother to Let Her Son Become a Cage Fighter

It does not happen overnight.

I started with wrestling at Bladensburg High School. Then I told my mom after a semester and a half of college that I was going to pursue fighting full time. I promised her if I failed, I would go back to school. She was worried, but she supported me. She even drove me to the fighters' house where I would live for the next couple of years to chase my dream.

I moved into that fighters' house and I was broke. I worked the kids' after-school program at the gym just to survive. The first year was rough. After the first year, something shifted. I went from not knowing HOW I was going to get to the UFC to knowing I WAS going to get to the UFC. It was just a matter of time.

And then it happened.

I went 9-0 as an amateur. Undefeated. I earned multiple championship belts. I signed with the UFC, the biggest fighting organization on the planet. I have competed on the biggest cards in UFC history, including UFC 300. I have earned over $100,000 in performance bonuses. I was featured during Black History Month by the UFC as someone "Paving the Path for the Next Generation." I even got to voice a character named Sunday Adelekan in Disney's Iwaju, the first Disney+ series centered on Nigerian culture and Lagos.

Not bad for a kid from Lagos who showed up in Maryland with nothing.

I grew up in this area. I trained in Maryland gyms my entire career. I know what is available here, and I know what has been missing. Until now.

For the teenager dealing with social anxiety who needs to discover what they are capable of. For the woman who wants to feel safe walking to her car at night. For the man who hasn't felt truly challenged in years.

I built this school because I know what martial arts did for a scared immigrant kid from Lagos. It gave me confidence. It gave me discipline. It gave me purpose. It gave me a career. It gave me the ability to support my 16 brothers and sisters. And I want to give that same gift to every family in Southern Maryland who wants more for themselves and their children.
